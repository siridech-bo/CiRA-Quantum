Executive summary

On 2026-07-01 we submitted a 6-node Max-Cut instance from the platform's example gallery to Origin Wukong 180-2 (WK_C180_2), a real superconducting QPU. The QPU ran 200 shots of a p = 1 QAOA circuit. The measurement distribution had a dominant mode on a bad bitstring, and the true optimum appeared in only 0.73% of shots. Nonetheless the platform correctly returned the optimum with objective value 8 — because the interpretation stage is a classical top-K filter that evaluates every measured bitstring against the polynomial and selects the winner by energy, not by frequency.

Finding: the "most probable" bitstring the QPU returned was 111111 at 33.6% probability, with a poor objective value of 2. The winning bitstring 101001 — objective value 8, the exact optimum — appeared with only 0.73% probability. Classical post-processing chose it correctly.

Problem instance

Max-Cut on a 6-node graph with edges: (0,1), (0,2), (1,3), (2,3), (2,4), (3,5), (4,5). Partition the nodes into two groups so that the number of edges between the groups is maximized.

Formulation the LLM emitted

The Claude (Sonnet 4.6) formulation produced 6 binary variables and zero constraints — the natural QAOA-friendly encoding (x_i + x_j - 2·x_i·x_j per edge). The linear coefficients Claude computed were slightly inflated (x_2 and x_3 came out as 4 instead of the ideal degree of 3), so this particular CQM's brute-force optimum is 8 rather than the textbook value of 7 for this graph. The validator flagged the disagreement in the approval panel; we approved anyway, since the point of the demo is QAOA behaviour, not LLM arithmetic.

The pedagogical insight

QAOA is a hybrid algorithm. Two steps, in order:

  1. Quantum step — Wukong 180-2 executed the variational circuit 200 times. Each execution collapses the entangled 6-qubit state to some 6-bit measurement. The distribution of those 200 measurements is biased toward good solutions, but at depth p = 1 the peak is not sharply concentrated on the optimum. Quantum noise (decoherence, gate infidelity, readout error) smears the distribution further.
  2. Classical step — for every distinct bitstring the QPU measured, the platform evaluates the polynomial at that bitstring and computes its exact objective value. This is a deterministic 6-bit arithmetic computation — no quantum uncertainty remains. The winner is the bitstring with the best objective value among all measured bitstrings.

The winner isn't the bitstring the QPU produced most often. The winner is the bitstring with the best classical objective — as long as the QPU produced it at least once.

Why the most-probable answer was wrong

111111 — "all nodes in the same group" — was measured 33.6% of the time. That's a bad Max-Cut answer: no edges cross between groups, and the objective evaluates to 2 (only from the linear terms that the LLM inflated). Yet it's the QPU's dominant mode because at p = 1 the phase separator doesn't yet strongly discourage same-group assignments, and the mixer (RX layer) doesn't have enough freedom to concentrate probability on the balanced bipartition.

Higher QAOA depth (p = 2, 3, …) would concentrate probability on the optimum. With p = 1 we get a rough gradient toward good solutions but still measure a lot of noise. The classical filter is the safety net — it doesn't care how noisy the quantum step is, as long as the optimum shows up at least once in the samples.

Why the winner is stable at only 0.73% probability

0.73% of 200 shots is roughly 1–2 measurements. The QPU produced 101001 a small number of times, and the classical filter recognized it as the optimum. The reliability question is: can we trust that the optimum will appear at all?

For this instance the answer is yes — the QAOA amplitude ridge covers the optimum and adjacent bitstrings, so at least one of them lands in the sample. For harder instances, the strategy is to increase the shot budget (say 1000 or 10000 shots) so rare-but-optimal bitstrings appear reliably.

Analogy for a non-technical audience

Imagine a biased die that lands on 6 (the best face) only 5% of the time, but every roll shows you which face came up. You roll 200 times. Even if 6 only appears 10 times, you write down "6" as your answer because you can see it's the best face. The die's bias helps — you don't roll uniformly — but the decision is made by you looking at what came up, not by whichever face came up most.

QAOA is that die. Wukong 180-2 is the physical roller. The classical top-K filter is you looking at the results.

Follow-up (2026-07-02): from bitstring to answer

A subsequent solve on the same platform used the Max Independent Set (5-Node) template — a natural follow-up demo for non-technical audiences. Claude's formulation lowered to a 10-qubit BQM, and the surprise was: why 10 qubits for a 5-node problem? The answer led to a clean explanation of how a real QPU's raw measurement gets turned into a plain-English answer, and how slack qubits (a QUBO-encoding trick) differ from quantum error correction (a hardware noise-protection technique). Both concepts add "extra qubits", but they exist for orthogonal reasons.

Why the 5-node problem used 10 qubits

The CQM Claude emitted had 5 logical binary variables (one per node, x_0…x_4) plus 5 inequality constraints — one per graph edge, enforcing "not both endpoints picked". When dimod.cqm_to_bqm lowered this CQM into a QUBO/Ising form suitable for QAOA, each inequality constraint required a slack qubit to soak up the "how far below the bound are we?" quantity. Five constraints → five slack qubits → 10 total qubits.

The four-stage interpretation pipeline

Every raw QPU measurement goes through the same pipeline before becoming a sentence the user can read:

Stage Input Output What it does
1. Measurement QAOA circuit on 10 qubits 10-bit string, e.g. 0010011101 Wukong collapses the entangled state; every rail is measured
2. Inversion Full BQM sample (logical + slack) CQM sample: {x_0:0, x_1:1, x_2:1, x_3:0, x_4:1} The invert() closure dimod returned during lowering drops the slack qubits and restores the original variable names
3. Registry lookup CQM sample + variable descriptions "Node 1 is chosen", "Node 2 is chosen", "Node 4 is chosen" The interpreter walks cqm.variables in order and pulls each description from the registry the LLM emitted
4. LLM summary Problem statement + technical listing "The largest independent set has 3 nodes: {1, 2, 4}." Claude Sonnet rewrites the technical output as a plain-English answer matching the user's question vocabulary

Key insight: the QPU has no notion of "some of these are the answer, others are internal". It measures every rail. The distinction between logical variables (the answer) and slack qubits (bookkeeping) is a compile-time construct that lives in the closure invert() captures. The QPU is oblivious.

Why the slack qubits matter even though we discard them

A reasonable question: if the slack qubits are thrown away, why bother? Because during QAOA optimization, they are what makes the energy landscape encode the constraint. A bitstring where the logical variables violate a non-adjacency constraint gets a big energy penalty through the slack coupling terms — so the QPU's probability distribution steers away from those states even at low circuit depth.

Concrete evidence: the top-K filter on this solve showed energies from −9 (winner) down to −97. The energy is computed over the full 10-qubit assignment (logical + slack) because that's what makes the ranking meaningful. If we only scored the 5 logical bits, all the "picked adjacent nodes" states would look identical to valid states.

Slack qubits vs. quantum error correction — a common confusion

Both add "extra qubits" beyond the logical minimum. Users new to quantum computing often conflate them because both appear as overhead in a qubit-count budget. But they exist at completely different layers of the stack, for orthogonal reasons.

Slack qubits Quantum error correction
Purpose Encode inequality constraints into a QUBO form the sampler can handle Protect a fragile quantum state against decoherence and gate errors
Where it lives Problem formulation / compile time Hardware or firmware / execution time
What it uses Logical binary variables (same kind as your problem variables) Physical qubits in structured code layouts (surface, Steane, qLDPC…)
Solver-agnostic? Yes — same trick used by classical SA, GPU SA, CP-SAT, D-Wave, and QAOA No — inseparable from a specific hardware model and its noise characterization
Overhead scale ~1 slack qubit per ≤ or ≥ inequality Dozens (Steane) to thousands (early surface code) of physical qubits per logical qubit
Failure mode An infeasible bitstring appears as a high-energy state; the classical filter never picks it Syndrome measurement decodes errors; if noise exceeds the code's threshold, correction fails silently
Example on Origin Wukong today The 5 slack qubits added to encode the 5 non-adjacency constraints in Max Independent Set Not exercised in this run — Wukong 180-2 is a near-term-noisy chip (NISQ), no QEC layer is engaged
Where it appears in this platform Optimization module, invisible to end users but visible in the preflight "lowered qubits" number qLDPC module (a separate app in the CiRA Quantum family) — designs, routes, and benchmarks quantum LDPC codes end-to-end
Both slack qubits and QEC add "extra qubits", but they do so for orthogonal reasons. Slack is problem-encoding overhead — a compile-time trick to make inequalities fit a QUBO. QEC is hardware noise-protection overhead — a runtime layer that detects and corrects errors on physical qubits. Same superficial symptom (bigger circuit than the logical problem asks for), completely different mechanism.

Where this matters for the demo narrative

When presenting to a research audience: the platform's slack qubit count is a formulation-quality signal. A tight formulation (like the natural-form Max-Cut, no auxiliary variables) lowers 1:1 from logical to BQM qubits. A loose one (like Claude's first Max Independent Set attempt with 5 inequality constraints) doubles the qubit budget through slack. That's an actionable insight — tighter formulations reach smaller QPUs. It has nothing to do with quantum error correction, which the platform's qLDPC module addresses in a separate track entirely.
