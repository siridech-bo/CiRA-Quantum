# Research note — Guo & Yang (2025), arXiv:2501.07387v2

> *"Efficient Quantum Circuit Compilation for Near-Term Quantum Advantage"*
> Yuchen Guo, Shuo Yang — Tsinghua University, Beijing
> Filed under: NISQ compilation, Riemannian optimization on unitaries
> Recorded: 2026-06-29 · siridech.bo@kmitl.ac.th

---

## TL;DR

The authors classically pre-compile a *deep* target circuit into a
*shallow brick-wall* layout of trainable single-qubit gates separated by
fixed CNOTs. The optimization happens on the **unitary manifold**
(Riemannian gradient projection), so every iterate is exactly unitary —
no re-parameterization through Euler angles, no normalization drift.

The headline number: **12.5× depth compression** on `ibm_brisbane`
(N = 12), extending reliable Ising dynamics from `d_target = 50` out to
`d_target = 160`. They additionally show that the optimal compiled depth
`d_max` is roughly **independent of system size N**, so total quantum
resources scale as **O(N)** — not O(N · target_depth) — for circuits
whose entanglement grows slowly.

## Method, in one diagram

```
target U  (deep, dense)               compiled Ũ  (shallow brick-wall)
┌──────────────────────┐              ┌───────────────────────────┐
│  d_target ≈ 50–160   │   classical  │  d ≈ 4–10                 │
│  arbitrary 2-qubit   │  ─────────►  │  trainable SU(2) per qubit│
│  gates               │   compile    │  + fixed CNOT staircase   │
└──────────────────────┘              └───────────────────────────┘
                                              ▲
                                              │ Riemannian gradient:
                                              │   ∇ᵢᴾ = ½ (Uᵢ†∇ᵢ − ∇ᵢ†Uᵢ)
                                              │   Uᵢ ← retract(Uᵢ − η ∇ᵢᴾ)
                                              │ (autodiff + Adam, MPO sim)
```

The loss balances two quantities:

| term                   | grows with…                             |
|------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| compilation error ε(d) | *smaller* d (less expressive shallow Ũ) |
| circuit-noise error    | *larger* d (more layers on the device)  |

→ There is an interior optimum `d_max` that **shifts left as hardware
gets noisier**, which is the surprising and useful part of the paper.

## Key contributions (verbatim claims, my notes)

1. **Brick-wall ansatz with fixed CNOTs.** Output is already at the
   ISA level — no further transpilation step needed. Claim works on CZ,
   ECR, and Mølmer–Sørensen native gates.
2. **Noise-aware objective.** Loss is not pure fidelity to U; it
   includes an estimate of the noise-induced infidelity at depth d.
   This is what makes "noisier hardware ⇒ shallower optimum" fall out.
3. **Riemannian optimization on U(2).** Single-qubit gates live on the
   2-dim complex unitary manifold. Projecting the Euclidean gradient
   into the tangent space at Uᵢ keeps every iterate unitary by
   construction — *no need to parameterize via 3 Euler angles*.
   * Projection: `∇ᵢᴾ = ½ (Uᵢ†∇ᵢ − ∇ᵢ†Uᵢ)` (skew-Hermitian part)
   * Retraction back to U(2): polar decomposition or QR.
4. **MPO-based simulation of the shallow target** for the loss
   computation. Bounds the classical cost of the compile pass; this is
   why the technique scales to N ≈ 30 qubits in classical pre-compute.
5. **Empirical results.**
   * Critical Ising, N = 10, τ = 0.1 → compression γ ∈ [7.5, 9] for
     `d_target ∈ [15, 20]`.
   * Hardware demo, N = 12 on `ibm_brisbane` → 12.5× compression at
     `d_target = 160`, validated against analytic σ₅ᶻσ₆ᶻ dynamics.
   * QFT → ~9.75× compression, beats analytic AQFT.
   * **Haar-random** circuits → γ ≈ 1.29 (essentially nothing), because
     entanglement grows ballistically and the shallow Ũ can't represent
     the target state at any reasonable depth.

## What I don't take at face value

* **Observable-restricted fidelity.** The IBM demo measures a single
  two-site ZZ correlator. That's a *much* weaker claim than full-state
  fidelity. The compiled circuit could be wildly off on other
  observables and the paper would still report success.
* **Compile cost is classical and not free.** They sweep MPO bond
  dimension and Adam iterations until convergence — the per-target
  compile time isn't reported in a way that lets us extrapolate to
  problem sizes our customers care about. The "O(N) quantum resources"
  claim says nothing about classical wall-clock to find the compilation.
* **N = 12 is small.** It's the right size for a methods paper. It's
  *not* the right size to base a product roadmap on. The interior
  optimum `d_max` may move discontinuously when going from
  superconducting (`ibm_brisbane`) to ion-trap fidelities.

## Design sketch — Riemannian SU(2) ansatz for the VQC trainer

### Where this would slot in

Current VQC ansatz [vqc.py:107-112](../../backend/app/qml/vqc.py#L107-L112)
uses `BasicEntanglerLayers` — one **scalar RY angle per qubit per layer**,
plus a ring of CNOTs. The trainable surface is
`(n_layers × n_qubits)` real numbers; gradient descent over Euler-angle
scalars is already implicitly on U(2) because RY(θ) is always unitary.

**So Riemannian projection adds nothing to the current ansatz.** The
technique becomes useful only if we *expand* the per-site parameterization
from a single RY to a *full* SU(2) (3 real DOF, captured as a 2×2 complex
matrix). That gives the optimizer access to RX/RY/RZ combinations
without re-deriving Euler chain-rule gradients by hand.

Proposed as an opt-in alternate ansatz, **not** a replacement for the
current educational one — the RY-only block is what makes the QML module
teachable.

### Concrete code shape

A new module `backend/app/qml/ansatz_su2.py` (sketch only):

```python
"""Per-qubit SU(2) brick-wall ansatz with Riemannian (Stiefel) updates.

Each "layer" = a row of trainable 2x2 unitaries (one per qubit) followed
by a fixed ring of CNOTs. Compared to BasicEntanglerLayers, this triples
the per-qubit DOF (1 → 3) and uses a Riemannian projection step instead
of vanilla Adam-over-angles. Following Guo & Yang (arXiv:2501.07387v2).
"""
from __future__ import annotations
import numpy as np

def riemannian_grad(U: np.ndarray, grad_eucl: np.ndarray) -> np.ndarray:
    """Project the Euclidean gradient onto the tangent space at U.

    Tangent space of U(n) at U is { U · A : A skew-Hermitian }, so the
    projection is U · skew(U† · grad). Equivalently ½(U†∇ − ∇†U)·U,
    which matches the form in eq. (4) of Guo & Yang.
    """
    A = U.conj().T @ grad_eucl
    skew = 0.5 * (A - A.conj().T)
    return U @ skew

def retract_polar(U: np.ndarray, step: np.ndarray) -> np.ndarray:
    """Map a tangent step back onto U(n) via polar decomposition.

    For SU(2) this is cheap: SVD a 2x2 matrix, throw away the singular
    values, multiply back. Numerically more robust than the Cayley map
    when the step is large.
    """
    W = U - step  # gradient descent direction
    Q, _, Vh = np.linalg.svd(W, full_matrices=False)
    return Q @ Vh

# ─── PennyLane integration ────────────────────────────────────────────
# Use qml.QubitUnitary(U, wires=k) for each per-qubit gate. The autograd
# interface returns dL/dU as a 2×2 complex matrix; we feed that into
# riemannian_grad(...) above instead of letting Adam touch it directly.
#
# Outer loop is hand-rolled (NOT qml.AdamOptimizer) because PennyLane's
# optimizer assumes Euclidean parameter space:
#
#   for epoch in range(n_epochs):
#       grads = qml.grad(loss_fn)(unitaries, bias, X_batch, y_batch)
#       for i, (U, g) in enumerate(zip(unitaries, grads)):
#           rgrad = riemannian_grad(U, g)
#           unitaries[i] = retract_polar(U, learning_rate * rgrad)
#       bias = bias - learning_rate * grad_bias  # bias is Euclidean
```

### Wiring into `trainer.py`

* Add a `VQCConfig.ansatz: Literal["basic_ry", "su2_brick"] = "basic_ry"`
  field — default keeps current behavior intact.
* `train_vqc(...)` branches on `config.ansatz`:
  * `"basic_ry"` → existing `AdamOptimizer` path (unchanged).
  * `"su2_brick"` → hand-rolled Riemannian loop calling
    `riemannian_grad` / `retract_polar` per gate per step.
* `CircuitInfo.entangler` reports `"SU(2) brick-wall (Riemannian, ring CNOT)"`
  so the frontend `VqcCircuitExplainer` can render the right label.

### What I'd want to validate before shipping

1. **Sanity test on `moons`.** With identical `(n_qubits, n_layers,
   n_epochs, learning_rate)`, the SU(2) ansatz should match-or-beat
   `basic_ry` test accuracy. If it underperforms, the retraction step
   size is wrong — likely too large for a 2×2 polar projection.
2. **Convergence comparison.** Plot loss-vs-epoch for both ansätze on
   `circles` (the hard one). Riemannian should converge in fewer epochs
   but each epoch is ~3× slower; net wall-clock unclear.
3. **Decision-boundary smoothness.** The 20×20 final-grid snapshot
   already in `compute_decision_grid` is a fine visual check —
   Riemannian boundaries should be *smoother* than vanilla Adam ones
   because the iterates never have to "correct" a non-unitary drift.

### What I'm explicitly NOT proposing

* Replacing `BasicEntanglerLayers` for the default educational path —
  it's pedagogically clearer for the "one feature per qubit" lesson.
* Adapting the **whole** Guo & Yang compile-then-run pipeline. That
  paper is about *dynamics simulation*, not classification. The
  Riemannian gradient trick is the transferable piece; the brick-wall
  compilation of a target unitary is not (there's no target unitary in
  VQC classification — only data and a loss).
* Hooking this into the QAOA path. QAOA's ansatz is parameterized by
  scalar βs and γs (cost-Hamiltonian time-evolution angles); SU(2) per
  qubit doesn't apply.

## Implementation log (2026-06-29)

### Step 1 — Manifold primitives (`backend/app/qml/ansatz_su2.py`)

Pure-numpy library: `riemannian_grad`, `retract_polar`, `is_unitary`,
`random_u2`, `init_near_identity`. ~95 LOC, no PennyLane/Flask imports.
Six unit tests in `backend/tests/test_qml_ansatz_su2.py` cover the
tangent-space property, retraction unitarity at step sizes 1e-3..10,
numerical-gradient agreement on a Frobenius loss, and end-to-end
convergence on a unitary-target toy problem. All green.

### Step 2 — Trainer wiring (`backend/app/qml/vqc.py`)

* `VQCConfig.ansatz: Literal["basic_ry", "su2_brick"] = "basic_ry"`.
* `TrainResult.su2_unitaries: list | None` — shape
  `(n_layers, n_qubits, 2, 2, 2)`, trailing axis `[real, imag]`.
* Early dispatch at the top of `train_vqc(...)`; the existing body is
  untouched, so the educational `basic_ry` path is bit-identical.
* New `_train_su2_brick(...)`: per-qubit `QubitUnitary` blocks + ring
  CNOTs, gradient via `qml.grad(...)`, Riemannian update per gate per
  step, plain SGD on the scalar bias.

absence of momentum-state bookkeeping makes it slightly faster in
practice on these small problems.

### Lesson from the implementation — autograd convention gotcha

Worth recording because anyone re-deriving this will hit it:

PennyLane's autograd interface (HIPS-autograd flavor) returns, for a
real-valued scalar loss `f(U)` over a complex matrix `U`, a gradient
`G` such that `df = Re tr(G^T · dU)`. **This is the conjugate of the
standard `∂f/∂U`.** The Euclidean steepest-ascent direction under the
real Frobenius inner product `<A, B>_R = Re tr(A† · B)` is therefore
`G.conj()`, not `G`.

Feeding raw `G` into `riemannian_grad(U, G)` does *gradient ascent*
on the loss. The smoke test caught this immediately: loss climbed
monotonically and accuracy fell below chance (i.e. the model learned
*anti-classification*). Conjugating the autograd output before the
manifold projection fixed it.

If we ever swap PennyLane for a JAX-based statevector simulator the
convention may flip — JAX returns the Wirtinger ∂f/∂U* directly, so
the conjugation step disappears.

### What Adam buys (and what it doesn't)

Adam-on-SU(2) was the apples-to-apples comparison the first sweep was
missing — `basic_ry` uses Adam internally, so the SGD-vs-Adam delta
inside `su2_brick` isolates the optimizer's contribution from the
ansatz's.

* **Adam consistently improves convergence speed.** `e@0.90` dropped
  9 → 4 on iris; `e@0.80` dropped 3 → 2 on moons. This is the
  textbook Adam benefit — adaptive per-parameter step size catches
  up to gates whose gradients sit at different scales.
* **Adam achieves lower final training loss everywhere.** The
  optimizer *is* better at minimizing the objective; this is not
  controversial.
* **Adam doesn't always help test accuracy.** On `wine` it
  *underperformed* by 15 points (test 0.61 vs SGD 0.76) while
  training loss was lower (0.587 vs 0.621) — textbook overfitting.
  Adam's stronger objective minimization on a small dataset finds
  minima that don't generalize.
* **Adam doesn't rescue ansatz-level weaknesses.** Where `su2_brick`
  lost to `basic_ry` (iris, breast_cancer), Adam recovered convergence
  speed but not final accuracy. The ceiling is set by the ansatz, not
  by the optimizer.

### Wall-time observation

`su2_brick` is consistently **20–40% faster** than `basic_ry` in wall
time. The per-step Adam state on SU(2) costs almost nothing on top of
SGD. The main cost driver is the autograd pass through `QubitUnitary`,
which is leaner than autograd through `BasicEntanglerLayers` + Adam's
optimizer-state bookkeeping.

### What changed in my read on this

**The 2-qubit teaser was misleading.** On `moons` and `circles`, SU(2)
wins clearly (and at `circles` it converges 4× faster). But at 4 qubits
on real data the story flattens:

* **Iris**: basic_ry *wins* by 4 points on test accuracy and converges
  twice as fast (e@0.9 = 5 vs 9). The data is separable enough that
  basic_ry's 9 params suffice; SU(2)'s 25 params give the optimizer
  extra room to overfit.
* **Wine**: SU(2) wins by 6 points on test, but train accuracy is
  lower — both are still in the "barely off chance" regime, so the
  win is fragile.
* **Breast cancer**: both stuck at 0.636 (≈ class imbalance). Neither
  converged at 15 epochs — this dataset needs more layers or epochs to
  separate basic_ry from SU(2) meaningfully.

**Wall time is consistently lower for SU(2)** (25–35% faster). The
hand-rolled Riemannian loop avoids `AdamOptimizer`'s setup-and-state
overhead, and that gap widens as samples × qubits grows.

### Revised product framing

SU(2) is **a research-tier feature, not a product-tier upgrade**.
* Customers asking *"can we try the Riemannian ansatz from Guo & Yang?"*
  → yes, ship it behind a tier-3 flag, comparable cost to basic_ry.
  Both SGD and Adam variants are wired through `VQCConfig.ansatz` and
  `VQCConfig.momentum`, persisted in the qml_jobs row, and folded
  into the `repro_hash` so distinct combinations don't collide in
  the archive.
* Marketing copy that says *"SU(2) brick-wall improves accuracy"* →
  honest only on toy non-linear 2-qubit data. Don't generalize.
* If we want to claim a real product-tier improvement, the next thing
  to vary is **n_layers**, not the ansatz or optimizer. SU(2) at 4
  qubits with more layers may pull ahead, but that's an additional
  sweep.

## What's left (deferred, not done)

* **Frontend exposure.** The route layer accepts `ansatz` and
  `momentum` end-to-end, but no UI radio exists yet. Add to the
  start-training form when a tier-3 customer drives the question.
  The `weights` field is `[]` for the SU(2) path — the
  weights-display table on the detail page should hide itself or
  show the `su2_unitaries` (n_layers, n_qubits, 2, 2, 2) tensor
  shape instead.
* **n_layers sweep.** The sweep above varies dataset + qubits but
  holds n_layers = 2. SU(2)'s extra per-qubit DOF may unlock more at
  deeper circuits where the entangler topology gets a chance to use
  the richer single-qubit bases. Defer until / unless we have a
  customer driving the question.
* **Adam on the scalar bias.** Currently vanilla SGD. Cheap to fix
  if convergence ever stalls on the bias — empirically it hasn't.
